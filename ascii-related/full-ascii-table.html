Brief History of ASCII Code

  1. Origins and Early Development:
    • In the 1960s, as computers began to communicate electronically, the need arose for a common character encoding format.
    • Before ASCII, different computer manufacturers used their own character sets, leading to compatibility issues.
    • In 1961, Bob Bemer of IBM proposed a common code, leading to the formation of the X3.4 committee.
    • On June 17, 1963, ASCII (American Standard Code for Information Interchange) was approved as the standard.
  2. Initial Challenges and Revisions:
    • Despite approval, ASCII didn't gain widespread acceptance due to IBM's use of EBCDIC (Extended Binary Coded Decimal Interchange Code) in its computers.
    • Revisions were issued in 1965 and 1967 to improve compatibility.
    • In 1968, U.S. President Lyndon B. Johnson mandated that federal systems be ASCII-compatible.
  3. ASCII's Impact and Evolution:
    • Originally developed for teleprinters, ASCII found broader application in personal computers (PCs) from 1981 onward.
    • ASCII uses seven-digit binary numbers, representing 128 different characters.
    • Its adoption paved the way for seamless communication across diverse computer systems.
